What is The Internet?

The Internet is a massive global network of interconnected computers and devices that communicate using standardized protocols. It allows billions of people to share information, communicate, and access services from anywhere in the world. The Internet is not owned by any single person or organization — it is a decentralized system built on shared infrastructure and open standards.

How the Internet Works

Data travels across the Internet in small packets. When you send an email or visit a website, your data is broken into packets, routed through various servers and routers, and then reassembled at the destination. This packet-switching system makes the Internet incredibly resilient — if one path is blocked, packets can take a different route.

History of the Internet

The Internet originated from ARPANET, a US military research project in the 1960s. It gradually evolved through the 1970s–1980s and became publicly accessible in the 1990s. The invention of the World Wide Web by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989 made it easy for everyday people to use the Internet through web browsers.

Why the Internet Matters

Today, the Internet underpins almost every aspect of modern life — from communication and education to commerce and entertainment. It has transformed how businesses operate, how people learn, and how societies connect across borders and cultures.
